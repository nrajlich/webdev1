Chronicles of the Medieval Table

Feasting Traditions

1347 – 1351
Event: The Great Famine and the Black Plague
Location: Europe
During this period, feasting habits changed drastically due to the famine and plague that swept across Europe. With limited resources, meals became simpler, often consisting of just bread and pottage. Nobility shifted to preserved foods, and spices became highly sought after for their ability to mask the flavor of less-than-fresh meats. This era highlighted a stark contrast between the meals of the wealthy and the peasantry.
1390
Event: The Publication of “The Forme of Cury”
Location: England
“The Forme of Cury,” a cookbook commissioned by King Richard II, was one of the earliest culinary manuscripts in England. It provided recipes for the royal court and included instructions on extravagant dishes such as “Blank Mang” and “Rysshews of Frute.” The book showcased the use of spices, sugar, and complex sauces, reflecting the sophistication of noble feasts during this period.

Culinary Lore

1347 – 1351
Institution: The Feast of St. George at Windsor Castle
Location: Windsor, England
This feast was one of the most elaborate gatherings of the medieval era, hosted by King Edward III. The grand dining hall was decorated with tapestries, and the tables were filled with exotic fruits, meats, and spices. The event reinforced the power of the monarchy, symbolizing wealth and influence. Attendees feasted on roasted peacocks, dressed in their feathers, and swans, a delicacy among nobility.
1500s
Institution: The Spread of Sugar in Europe
Location: France and Italy
Dessert Evolution
Sugar became a status symbol in the late Middle Ages, used to create elaborate edible sculptures, confections, and sweets. This period saw the beginning of sugar’s popularity among the elite, who indulged in candied fruits and marzipan sculptures. It marked a transition in the culinary world, as desserts began to hold a special place in noble feasts.
